The Math, Simplified

Understanding the core calculations without the complexity

üìà Momentum (Trend Following) 

Momentum = (Price Today / Price 252 Days Ago) - 1

Example:
If Apple was $100 a year ago and is $120 today:
Momentum = (120 / 100) - 1 = 0.20 = +20%

‚úÖ Positive momentum = upward trend
‚ùå Negative momentum = downward trend

Why this matters: Stocks with strong momentum tend to keep moving in the same direction (Newton's first law for stocks!)

üìä Volatility (Risk Measure) 

Volatility = Standard Deviation of Returns √ó ‚àö252

What it measures:
- How much prices jump around each day
- Higher volatility = more unpredictable
- Lower volatility = more stable

Example:

Daily moves of ¬±3.0% = High Volatility

Why this matters: High volatility = more risk but also more opportunity (or danger!)

üßÆ The Z-Score: Comparing Current to Normal

Formula: Z-Score = (Current Value - Average) / Standard Deviation

Translation: "How unusual is today's value compared to normal?"

Examples:

‚Ä¢ Z-Score = 2 ‚Üí Higher than 95% of historical values (unusual!)

Glossary: Terms Demystified

A plain-English guide to quantitative finance jargon

Alpha

A signal or pattern that predicts future returns. "Finding alpha" means discovering something the market hasn't priced in yet.

Sharpe Ratio

Return per unit of risk. Formula: (Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Volatility. Higher is better. Above 1.0 is good, above 2.0 is excellent.

Maximum Drawdown

The biggest peak-to-trough loss. If you invested $100 and it dropped to $75 before recovering, that's a 25% drawdown.

Value-at-Risk (VaR)

The maximum loss you'd expect 95% of the time. VaR of 10% means "there's only a 5% chance you'll lose more than 10%."

CVaR (Conditional VaR)

The average loss in the worst 5% of outcomes. Answers: "If things go bad, HOW bad?" More useful than VaR for tail risk.

Walk-Forward Validation

Training method that simulates real trading: always test on future data you haven't seen. Prevents "backtesting overfitting".

Feature Engineering

Creating useful inputs for ML models. Example: turning raw prices into "momentum", "volatility", and "mean reversion" signals.

Regime Classification

Identifying what "state" the market is in. Like weather: sunny (low vol), cloudy (normal), or stormy (high vol).

SHAP Values

Explainability metric from game theory. Shows each feature's contribution to a prediction. The "why" behind the "what".

Cross-Sectional

Comparing assets to each other at the same point in time. Example: "AAPL has stronger momentum than MSFT today."

Bollinger Bands

Volatility indicator showing price channels. When price hits upper band = potentially overbought. Lower band = potentially oversold.

RSI (Relative Strength)

Momentum oscillator (0-100). Above 70 = overbought (potential reversal down). Below 30 = oversold (potential reversal up).
